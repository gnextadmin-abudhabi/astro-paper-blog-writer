Few food writers have captured the internet's imagination quite like Marilyn Hagerty. A columnist for the *Grand Forks Herald* in North Dakota, Hagerty spent decades reviewing the restaurants her neighbors actually ate at — not Michelin-starred temples of gastronomy, but the Olive Gardens, the family diners, and the local lunch counters that form the backbone of everyday American dining. When her 2012 review of a newly opened Grand Forks Olive Garden went viral, the world discovered something the people of North Dakota already knew: honest, warm, and genuinely useful writing about food is timeless.

Her collected reviews, published in the book *Grand Forks*, are more than a charming cultural artifact. For restaurant owners navigating the modern landscape of online reviews, Hagerty's work offers a surprisingly practical education. She wrote for real diners, about real concerns, in plain language — and that, it turns out, is exactly what the internet rewards too.

## Who Was Marilyn Hagerty, and Why Does She Matter?

Hagerty didn't chase trends or cultivate an air of culinary superiority. She reviewed the middle market — the places where working families celebrated birthdays, where retirees caught up over coffee, where the food didn't need to be revolutionary, just good. Her philosophy was rooted in community journalism: be honest, be fair, and never forget that the restaurant you're critiquing is someone's livelihood and your neighbor's livelihood too.

That approach didn't make her soft. It made her clear-eyed. She assessed what mattered to her readers, not what impressed food critics in New York or San Francisco. And when her Olive Garden review went viral — met with equal parts irony and genuine affection — it wasn't because she was naive. It was because she was authentic in a media environment that had largely forgotten how to be.

## What Diners Actually Care About (According to Hagerty)

Reading through *Grand Forks*, a picture emerges of what everyday diners genuinely notice and remember. These aren't abstract values — they're specific, sensory, and deeply human concerns.

- **Attentive but not excessive wait staff.** Hagerty noticed when servers were present without being overbearing — a balance that remains one of the most commonly cited factors in modern restaurant reviews.
- **Clean bathrooms and facilities.** She mentioned restroom cleanliness more than once, understanding that diners read it as a signal of the kitchen's standards.
- **Quality napkins for messy meals.** A small detail, yes — but the right napkin at a rib dinner communicates that the restaurant has thought about the experience from the diner's perspective.
- **Honest menu descriptions.** Hagerty appreciated menus that told you what you were actually getting. Overpromising and underdelivering was, for her, a minor betrayal.
- **Cold water in good glasses.** Again, the details. A meal begins the moment you sit down, and Hagerty paid attention to how restaurants set that tone.
- **Fair pricing and genuine value.** She highlighted specials and lunch deals with enthusiasm, because her readers needed to know whether a meal was worth the drive and the spend.

None of these are revolutionary insights. But that's precisely the point. What Hagerty understood — and what the best online reviewers today reflect — is that diners aren't grading restaurants against an abstract ideal. They're asking a simpler question: *Did this place take care of me?*

## Bridging Hagerty's World and the Modern Review Landscape

Here's where Hagerty's old-school journalism philosophy connects directly to local SEO and online reputation strategy. The diners leaving Google and Yelp reviews today are asking exactly the same questions Hagerty was answering in her column. The platform has changed. The human need hasn't.

Research consistently shows that **98% of consumers read local business reviews** before making a decision about where to eat, shop, or spend their money. And the single greatest motivator for leaving a positive review? A genuinely great experience. Not a discount. Not a nudge. The experience itself.

This is Hagerty's lesson translated into digital terms: earn the review by earning the moment.

### Strategy 1: Reward Great Experiences — By Creating Them

The most reliable review strategy isn't a clever follow-up email sequence. It's a clean bathroom, an attentive server, and a meal that delivers what the menu promised. Train your team to care about the details Hagerty noticed — because those are the details your guests are noticing too.

When the experience is genuinely good, asking for a review becomes natural rather than awkward. A brief, sincere request — "We'd love to hear what you thought" — lands very differently after a meal that delivered than it does as a transactional afterthought.

### Strategy 2: Encourage Specific, Detailed Feedback

Google now surfaces structured review content — food quality, atmosphere, service, wait times, cleanliness — alongside traditional star ratings. Reviews that speak to these specific elements carry more weight algorithmically and more persuasive power for potential customers.

When you invite guests to share their experience, be specific about what you'd love them to address. Did they notice the atmosphere? The speed of service? Encourage them to include photos or short videos — these significantly increase the credibility and visibility of a review. Think of it as helping them write the kind of review Hagerty would have written: useful, specific, and honest.

### Strategy 3: Maintain Absolute Ethical Standards

This is where Hagerty's journalistic integrity translates most directly. She never accepted compensation that would compromise her honesty. Her credibility was built on the trust of her readers — and she protected it.

For restaurant owners, the equivalent is straightforward: don't incentivize reviews in exchange for discounts, don't gate reviews by steering unhappy customers away from public platforms, don't create fake accounts, and don't engage in review spam. These practices violate the guidelines of every major review platform and, in a growing number of jurisdictions, legal standards around consumer deception.

More practically: they don't work. Savvy diners spot inauthenticity. Algorithms increasingly detect it. And the reputational damage from being caught far outweighs any short-term rating bump.

## The Real Lesson From Grand Forks

Marilyn Hagerty became internet-famous because she was honest in a way the internet wasn't expecting. She wrote about cold water in good glasses and clean napkins with the same care a critic might bring to a tasting menu — because to her readers, those things mattered just as much.

The lesson for restaurant owners seeking authentic reviews is the same one that made her work endure: **online fame is not a substitute for offline trust.**

The five-star review you want isn't written in your marketing strategy. It's written in the moment a first-time guest walks in and feels, without quite being able to articulate why, that someone here has thought about their experience. It's written in the training session where a manager explains why clean restrooms matter. It's written in the menu that says what it means.

Build that, and the reviews will follow. Just like they followed Hagerty's readers to the restaurants she loved.
