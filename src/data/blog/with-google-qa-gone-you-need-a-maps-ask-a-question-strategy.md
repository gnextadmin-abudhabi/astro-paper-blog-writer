If you've been relying on Google's Q&A feature to manage how potential customers get answers about your business, it's time to rethink your approach. Google quietly deprecated its longstanding Q&A feature — which had been a fixture of Google Business Profiles since 2017 — and replaced it with something fundamentally different: **Ask A Question (AAQ)**. Rolled out in February 2024 within the Google Maps app, this new feature shifts the power dynamic between businesses and the AI that now speaks on their behalf. And if you're not prepared, the consequences could range from missed opportunities to outright misinformation circulating about your brand.

Here's what you need to know — and what you need to do about it.

## What Changed and Why It Matters

The original Q&A feature was user-generated. Customers and business owners could post questions and answers directly on a Google Business Profile, creating a living FAQ that businesses could monitor and contribute to. It wasn't perfect, but it gave businesses a meaningful seat at the table.

AAQ is a different beast entirely. Instead of surfacing user-generated responses, it uses generative AI to scrape and synthesize information from across your digital footprint — your business profile, website, reviews, photos, menus, and more — to generate answers on the fly. The user asks a question in the Google Maps app, and AI constructs a response based on whatever data it can find.

That's a significant shift. You're no longer answering questions directly. The AI is — using your data, interpreted through its own logic.

## Where AAQ Currently Lives (and Where It Doesn't)

Before diving into strategy, it's worth understanding the current boundaries of this feature:

- **Google Maps app only** — AAQ does not appear on Google Maps desktop or in standard search results
- **Primarily a US market feature** — international rollout is limited at this stage
- **Excluded from regulated categories** — businesses in medical, legal, and locksmith categories are notably absent from AAQ, likely due to liability concerns around AI-generated guidance in sensitive domains

This means AAQ isn't everywhere yet. But given Google's trajectory with AI integration, assuming it will stay limited is a risk not worth taking.

## The Core Problem: You've Lost Direct Authority

The most significant business implication here is a loss of control. With the old Q&A, you could post answers yourself, flag misinformation, and shape the narrative. With AAQ, the AI draws its own conclusions. If your business data is incomplete, outdated, or scattered across inconsistent sources, the AI might generate answers that are inaccurate, misleading, or simply wrong.

Generative AI is notoriously prone to hallucination — confidently stating things that aren't true. Apply that tendency to questions like *"Is this restaurant open on Mondays?"* or *"Do they offer wheelchair-accessible parking?"* and you can see how the stakes get real very quickly.

Worse, liability around AI-generated misinformation remains murky. If a customer acts on an incorrect AI-generated answer about your business and has a negative experience, the path to accountability is unclear. That's a problem for businesses and consumers alike.

## Your AAQ Strategy: Feed the Algorithm Right

Since you can't directly write the answers anymore, your job becomes ensuring the AI has accurate, rich, and well-structured information to work from. Think of it as optimizing your business's data ecosystem so the AI has no reason to guess — or get it wrong.

### 1. Keep Your Core Business Information Airtight

This is non-negotiable. Your business name, address, phone number, and hours of operation must be accurate and consistent across your Google Business Profile and every other platform where your business appears. Inconsistencies confuse both users and algorithms.

### 2. Upload Rich Visual and Menu Content

Photos, videos, and menus aren't just for aesthetics — they're data sources the AI draws from. A well-stocked profile with up-to-date imagery and menu details gives the AI more accurate material to synthesize answers from, especially for questions about atmosphere, offerings, or pricing.

### 3. Encourage Detailed, Descriptive Customer Reviews

Generic five-star reviews don't feed the algorithm much. Detailed reviews that mention specific services, products, accessibility features, or experiences give the AI substantive content to reference. Encourage satisfied customers to write specific, honest reviews — and consider including photos.

### 4. Optimize Your Business Profile Attributes

Google Business Profile attributes — things like "women-owned," "outdoor seating," "free Wi-Fi," or "accepts credit cards" — are prime data points for AAQ. Make sure yours are fully filled out and regularly reviewed for accuracy as your business evolves.

### 5. Build FAQ Content on Your Website

Since AAQ pulls from your website, consider adding a dedicated FAQ section that addresses the questions customers commonly ask. Structure it clearly with direct questions and concise answers. This gives the AI a pre-packaged resource to draw from, reducing the chance it fabricates an answer.

### 6. Link Your Social Media Profiles

Your social media presence is part of your broader data footprint. Ensuring your Google Business Profile links to active, accurate social accounts helps the AI triangulate consistent information and presents a more complete picture of your business.

### 7. Verify Your Business Categorization

Being categorized correctly in Google Business Profile influences what questions get asked — and answered — about your business. An incorrectly categorized business may receive irrelevant questions or generate AI answers based on the wrong assumptions.

## The Silver Lining: AAQ Can Work in Your Favor

It's not all bad news. For businesses that take the time to build a comprehensive digital profile, AAQ offers some genuine advantages.

Users get faster, more nuanced answers than a basic FAQ would provide. Someone asking a layered question — *"Do you have vegan options and is the space family-friendly?"* — can receive a synthesized response that draws on reviews, menu data, and attributes simultaneously. That's powerful for conversion.

Small businesses with limited websites also stand to benefit. The AI aggregates from multiple sources, meaning a business with an active Google profile, strong reviews, and consistent social presence can punch above its weight compared to competitors with more elaborate — but outdated — websites.

## Act Now, Not Later

The window to get ahead of this is open, but it won't stay that way. As AAQ expands beyond the Maps app and potentially into desktop and international markets, the volume of AI-generated answers about your business will grow. Every piece of missing, outdated, or inconsistent data is an invitation for the AI to fill in the blanks — and it may not fill them correctly.

Your Google Business Profile is no longer just a listing. It's the primary source material for an AI that speaks about your business to potential customers every day. Treat it that way.

Audit your profile this week. Update your hours, refresh your photos, review your attributes, and draft that FAQ page you've been putting off. The businesses that thrive in the AAQ era won't be the ones who wait to see how it plays out — they'll be the ones who made sure the AI had nothing but good things to say.
